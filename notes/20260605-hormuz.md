# Strait of Hormuz — simulation design notes

June 2026. Planning document for the second major Orbital scenario.

---

## Why this matters

Almost every article in the press about the Hormuz crisis is static. Readers are
told that fertilizer prices rose 80%, that 45 million more people face starvation,
that helium is evaporating in stranded cryogenic containers. They absorb facts but
cannot interrogate them. They cannot ask: what if the strait reopened next month?
What if India activated its strategic reserves? What if fertilizer prices only rose
40% — does that halve the impact, or is there a threshold below which the cascade
doesn't trigger?

This simulation will be the first interactive model of this crisis available to
lay users. No institution publishing on this — FAO, IEA, S&P Global, Kpler — has
made their models accessible to non-specialists. The closest analogue is what the
IPCC scenario does for climate: takes expert findings and makes them explorable
rather than merely legible.

The political angle requires care. The model must not take sides on the conflict.
It presents conditional scenarios: "if the strait remains at X% blockade for Y
months, here are the projected commodity price trajectories and population
exposure." Framing is systemic and economic, not military or political.

---

## The core problem: what kind of model is this?

The Hormuz scenario is fundamentally different from the IPCC temperature model.
IPCC is a **physical field model**: one global temperature anomaly distributed
geographically. Hormuz is a **network flow model**: commodities move through
nodes (wells, ports, refineries, storage, consumers) along edges (shipping lanes,
pipelines), and disruption at one node propagates downstream.

This distinction shapes everything: the agent types, the data needed, the
visualization, and how the model generalises to other crises.

## What flows (the commodities)

These are the "molecules" being inhibited. Each is a separate commodity type with
distinct physical and economic characteristics:

| Commodity | Gulf share of global trade | Key characteristic |
|---|---|---|
| Crude oil | ~20% of world supply | Storable, multiple substitute routes possible |
| LNG (liquefied natural gas) | ~25% of world LNG trade | Qatar dominant; requires specialized tankers |
| Urea (fertilizer) | ~41% of global seaborne urea | Seasonal — spring planting window is critical |
| Ammonia | ~28% of global seaborne ammonia | Precursor to nitrogen fertilizers |
| Sulfur | ~50% of global seaborne sulfur | Required for phosphate fertilizer production |
| Helium | ~33% of global supply (Qatar) | Cannot substitute; boils off in ~35–48 days in stranded containers |
| Methanol | Major regional producer | Feedstock for plastics, synthetic fabrics |

**Helium is the sharpest case study** in non-obvious supply chain logic: it's
irreplaceable for semiconductor production and MRI machines, it has a hard
physical deadline (boil-off), and it's invisible to most people until chip
fabrication stops.

## The geographic nodes (infrastructure to map)

These are the real-world physical entities with known geographic positions that
form the backbone of the model and the visualization. Data exists for all of them.

### Producers / origin
- **Oil and gas fields**: Saudi Aramco (Ghawar, Khurais, Shaybah, Safaniya
  offshore), Qatar's North Field (world's largest single gas field), UAE (ADNOC
  Abu Dhabi offshore), Kuwait (Burgan), Iraq (Rumaila, Kirkuk), Iran fields.
- **Fertilizer complexes**: Qatar Fertiliser Company (QAFCO) at Ras Laffan,
  SABIC plants in Saudi Arabia, ADNOC in UAE.
- **LNG terminals**: Qatar's Ras Laffan complex (world's largest LNG export
  facility), UAE Das Island, Oman LNG.

### Chokepoints / routes
- **Strait of Hormuz** — 21 miles wide, 21km navigable channel; the central
  parameter of this model.
- **Suez Canal** — alternative for oil heading to Europe; adds ~7 days and
  significant cost; has its own capacity limits.
- **Cape of Good Hope** — full alternative for Asia-bound oil; adds ~2–3 weeks.
- **Saudi East-West Pipeline** (Petroline) — 1.2 Mbpd capacity; bypasses Hormuz
  for Saudi oil headed to the Red Sea. Currently the most important safety valve.
- **Iraq-Turkey (Kirkuk-Ceyhan) Pipeline** — 0.35 Mbpd; bypasses Hormuz for
  Iraqi oil to Mediterranean. Damaged and intermittently operational.
- **Abu Dhabi-Fujairah Pipeline** — 1.5 Mbpd; UAE bypass to Gulf of Oman coast.
  The most strategically significant recent investment.

### Major destination ports and refineries
Key refinery clusters: Rotterdam (Europe's largest), Ulsan/Yeosu (South Korea),
Jamnagar (India, world's largest single complex), Jurong (Singapore), Chiba
(Japan), Tianjin (China). These are the consumption-side endpoints whose supply
is disrupted.

Each of these has a known lat/lon and can be a geographic node in the simulation
and on the visualization globe.

## Agent taxonomy

### Tier 1: Infrastructure agents (geographically located)
These are initialized from reference data and are largely static:

- `OilField` — production capacity (Mbpd), current output (% of capacity), operator
- `GasField` — similar; distinguishes pipeline gas vs LNG
- `Port` — lat/lon, commodity types handled, daily throughput capacity, current congestion
- `Refinery` — lat/lon, crude input capacity, output products, current utilization
- `Pipeline` — origin/destination nodes, capacity (Mbpd), current operational status
- `FertilizerPlant` — lat/lon, input requirements (natural gas, sulfur), output capacity, current status
- `StorageFacility` — strategic petroleum reserve, tank farm; capacity + current fill level

### Tier 2: Market agents (pricing and routing logic)
- `CrudeOilMarket` — Brent/WTI/Dubai Mercantile spot prices; demand/supply balance
- `LNGMarket` — JKM (Japan-Korea Marker) spot price; regional differentiation
- `FertilizerMarket` — urea, ammonia, phosphate prices; seasonal demand curves
- `InsuranceMarket` — war-risk premium as a function of blockade intensity and
  incident rate; this is the most non-obvious agent: even a 30% physical blockade
  can generate an effective 90%+ commercial blockade via uninsurable risk
- `ShippingMarket` — Baltic Dry Index / tanker day rates; derived from supply/demand
  for vessel capacity on available routes

### Tier 3: Geopolitical scenario parameters (not modelled as agents)
Deliberately treated as **parameters, not agents**, to avoid political modelling:
- `BlockadeIntensity` (0–1) — physical effectiveness of Iranian maritime interdiction
- `InsuranceMultiplier` — amplification factor from insurance freeze
- `USNavyEscortCoverage` (0–1) — fraction of commercial traffic that can be escorted
- `PipelineBypassCapacity` — how much of East-West, Fujairah pipelines are online

The user sets these parameters (or the simulation steps them over time) and the
model shows consequences without simulating the decision-making of any state actor.

### Tier 4: Consumer/impact agents (second-order effects)
- `Country` — the core second-order agent:
  - Import dependency coefficients: oil, LNG, urea, ammonia, sulfur, helium
  - Strategic reserve levels (oil: IEA mandates 90-day supply; fertilizer: minimal)
  - Population size, food import dependency, GDP per capita
  - Current stress state: derived from commodity price × import dependency
- `FarmingRegion` — optional sub-country resolution: fertilizer application rate
  as a function of input price; yield loss as a function of reduced application
  using FAO elasticity coefficients
- `FertilizerPlant` — demand for natural gas input; shuts down when LNG price
  exceeds threshold (real shutdowns observed in India, Bangladesh, Pakistan)

## Aggregate flow model vs. geographic granularity

The user's question: can we have both an aggregate flow model AND geographic
visualization? Yes — and these are not in tension. The architecture is:

1. **Aggregate commodity flows** as the computational model: oil flows from
   Saudi fields to Rotterdam at X Mbpd, disrupted to Y Mbpd when Hormuz is
   at Z% blockade. The arithmetic is simple and fast.

2. **Geographic nodes** as visualization anchors: each flow has a real origin
   point (Ras Tanura, lat 26.64°N, lon 50.17°E) and a real destination
   (Rotterdam, lat 51.89°N, lon 4.14°E). The arc drawn on the globe between
   them is the visualization of an aggregate flow — its thickness and brightness
   represent the volume, its color the commodity type.

This gives lay users exactly what they want — visual confirmation that there are
real oil fields, real refineries, real chokepoints — without requiring individual
tanker simulation. The nodes are real, the flows are real (initialized from UN
Comtrade bilateral trade data), and the disruption is parameterized.

**Visualization intent (for later implementation):**
- 3D globe (extending the existing Three.js Globe component)
- Arcs between origin and destination nodes: radius proportional to flow volume,
  animated particles moving along arcs
- Node markers: oil fields (amber), LNG terminals (blue), refineries (orange),
  ports (white), fertilizer plants (green), strategic reserves (grey)
- Chokepoint markers: pulsing ring at Hormuz, Suez, Cape; size = current
  blockade intensity
- Country fill: choropleth coloring by stress index (food security, economic
  impact)
- Time control: slider from pre-crisis baseline through escalation to various
  resolution scenarios

Individual tanker visualization would be a later enhancement using AIS data —
interesting but not required for the model to produce meaningful and visually
compelling results.

## Other modelling efforts to learn from

**FAO/AMIS** — publishes methodology for fertilizer shock cascades. Their approach
is input-output (static): fertilizer price → application rate reduction (elasticity
~0.3–0.6) → yield reduction → food price increase → food insecurity. Scavengeable:
the elasticity coefficients and their country-level vulnerability indices.

**Kpler / Vortexa** — the most operationally relevant. They track individual
vessel movements and publish aggregate commodity flow analytics. Their output is
what journalists cite. They don't publish models, but the data is commercially
available and some aggregate statistics appear in free reports.

**S&P Global Commodity Insights** (Platts/IHS Markit) — most sophisticated
commercial oil flow models. Methodology described in accessible terms in their
market reports. The structure: supply nodes → route nodes → demand nodes, with
price-clearing at each step.

**GTAP (Global Trade Analysis Project)** — the academic workhorse for trade shock
analysis. A CGE (Computable General Equilibrium) model covering country × sector
bilateral trade. The GTAP database gives us the input-output coefficients (how
much oil goes into fertilizer production, how much fertilizer goes into agriculture)
that should seed the Country agents. The model itself is heavy, but its structure
is directly applicable.

**Oxford Economics / Capital Economics** — publish macro shock models for oil
price spikes. Their GDP elasticity estimates (% GDP impact per $10/barrel oil
price increase) are useful calibration inputs.

The gap all these efforts share: none of them are interactive. FAO publishes
a PDF. Kpler publishes a dashboard readable by analysts. GTAP runs in a
specialized software package. The Orbital simulation makes the same underlying
mechanics explorable by anyone.

## The general second-order model concept

Both IPCC warming and Hormuz closure converge on the same second-order variables:
- Food price index
- Fuel price index
- Fertilizer availability
- Economic stress
- Population vulnerability

The architectural insight: rather than duplicate the human-impact modeling in
each scenario, build a **Global Stress Layer** as a reusable module:

```
Scenario A (IPCC warming)     ─┐
Scenario B (Hormuz closure)   ─┼─▶  [ Global Stress Layer ]  ─▶  Population outcomes
Scenario C (future scenario)  ─┘
```

The stress layer takes commodity price indices and availability signals as
inputs and produces food insecurity levels, migration pressure, and political
stability indicators by country. FAO and WFP publish calibration data for this
layer (their food insecurity thresholds and price-hunger elasticities).

This generalises the simulation engine: the Hormuz model is one "forcing" applied
to a general global system, exactly as CO₂ forcing is applied to the climate system.

## On tipping points and meaningful feedback

The user raises the hardest problem: connecting quantitative outputs (5°C warming,
80% fertilizer price increase) to human meaning (deaths, migration, collapse).

Thresholds that matter — informed by existing frameworks:
- **Food insecurity threshold**: WFP uses a food basket cost index; above ~30%
  price increase, acute hunger risk rises sharply in high-vulnerability countries
- **Nitrogen fertilizer application**: below ~60% of normal application, cereal
  yields typically drop 20–40% (FAO elasticity data)
- **Oil price spike**: each $20/barrel sustained increase correlates to ~0.5%
  global GDP reduction (IMF estimates)
- **State fragility**: the Fund for Peace Fragile States Index includes food/fuel
  price shocks as explicit inputs; there are historical correlations between price
  spikes and political instability events

These thresholds can be modelled as tipping functions in Country agents —
continuous responses that cross visible thresholds at calibrated values.

## Open questions for planning

- [ ] What is the time resolution? Daily for the crisis acute phase (tanker
  movements), monthly for second-order effects (farming, hunger)?
- [ ] Do we model the current actual 2026 crisis (needing real recent data) or
  a parameterized generic Hormuz closure scenario (more flexible, less data work)?
  Suggest: parameterized scenario with real reference infrastructure, validated
  against observed price data.
- [ ] What is the first visible output? Suggest: world map with country-level
  oil-import stress coloring, plus commodity price time series. Fast to build,
  immediately legible.
- [ ] Database question: UN Comtrade data for ~200 countries × ~20 commodity types
  is a few MB as JSON — still filesystem-tractable. Hold on MongoDB until the
  reference data volume or real-time update requirements demand it.
- [ ] How to handle the political sensitivity? Framing: conditional scenario
  analysis ("if blockade at 80% for 6 months..."), historical precedent anchor
  (1980s Tanker Wars), no state actor blame, transparent model assumptions.

## Proposed build sequence

**Phase 1 — Static geography + commodity flows**
- Load infrastructure nodes from Global Energy Monitor + World Port Index
- Load country import dependency from UN Comtrade + World Bank
- Build commodity flow network (producers → routes → consumers)
- Parameterize Hormuz blockade intensity
- Output: country-level price impact + stress index

**Phase 2 — Second-order cascades**
- Fertilizer price → application rate → yield reduction (FAO elasticities)
- Food price → food insecurity by country (WFP thresholds)
- Strategic reserve depletion over time

**Phase 3 — Geographic visualization**
- Globe arcs for commodity flows (volume = thickness/brightness, commodity = color)
- Node markers for infrastructure (fields, ports, refineries, plants)
- Country choropleth for stress index
- Time slider / blockade intensity control

**Phase 4 — Validation + AIS data**
- Validate commodity price trajectories against observed 2026 price data
- Optionally layer in AIS tanker data for strait transit visualization
