# Strait of Hormuz — simulation design notes

June 2026. Planning document for the second major Orbital scenario.

---

## Why this matters

Almost every article in the press about the Hormuz crisis is static. Readers are
told that fertilizer prices rose 80%, that 45 million more people face starvation,
that helium is evaporating in stranded cryogenic containers. They absorb facts but
cannot interrogate them. They cannot ask: what if the strait reopened next month?
What if India activated its strategic reserves? What if fertilizer prices only rose
40% — does that halve the impact, or is there a threshold below which the cascade
doesn't trigger?

This simulation will be the first interactive model of this crisis available to
lay users. No institution publishing on this — FAO, IEA, S&P Global, Kpler — has
made their models accessible to non-specialists. The closest analogue is what the
IPCC scenario does for climate: takes expert findings and makes them explorable
rather than merely legible.

The political angle requires care. The model must not take sides on the conflict.
It presents conditional scenarios: "if the strait remains at X% blockade for Y
months, here are the projected commodity price trajectories and population
exposure." Framing is systemic and economic, not military or political.

---

## The core problem: what kind of model is this?

The Hormuz scenario is fundamentally different from the IPCC temperature model.
IPCC is a **physical field model**: one global temperature anomaly distributed
geographically. Hormuz is a **network flow model**: commodities move through
nodes (wells, ports, refineries, storage, consumers) along edges (shipping lanes,
pipelines), and disruption at one node propagates downstream.

This distinction shapes everything: the agent types, the data needed, the
visualization, and how the model generalises to other crises.

---

## What flows (the commodities)

These are the "molecules" being inhibited. Each is a separate commodity type with
distinct physical and economic characteristics:

| Commodity | Gulf share of global trade | Key characteristic |
|---|---|---|
| Crude oil | ~20% of world supply | Storable, multiple substitute routes possible |
| LNG (liquefied natural gas) | ~25% of world LNG trade | Qatar dominant; requires specialized tankers |
| Urea (fertilizer) | ~41% of global seaborne urea | Seasonal — spring planting window is critical |
| Ammonia | ~28% of global seaborne ammonia | Precursor to nitrogen fertilizers |
| Sulfur | ~50% of global seaborne sulfur | Required for phosphate fertilizer production |
| Helium | ~33% of global supply (Qatar) | Cannot substitute; boils off in ~35–48 days in stranded containers |
| Methanol | Major regional producer | Feedstock for plastics, synthetic fabrics |

**Helium is the sharpest case study** in non-obvious supply chain logic: it's
irreplaceable for semiconductor production and MRI machines, it has a hard
physical deadline (boil-off), and it's invisible to most people until chip
fabrication stops.

## The geographic nodes (infrastructure to map)

These are the real-world physical entities with known geographic positions that
form the backbone of the model and the visualization. Data exists for all of them.

### Producers / origin
- **Oil and gas fields**: Saudi Aramco (Ghawar, Khurais, Shaybah, Safaniya
  offshore), Qatar's North Field (world's largest single gas field), UAE (ADNOC
  Abu Dhabi offshore), Kuwait (Burgan), Iraq (Rumaila, Kirkuk), Iran fields.
- **Fertilizer complexes**: Qatar Fertiliser Company (QAFCO) at Ras Laffan,
  SABIC plants in Saudi Arabia, ADNOC in UAE.
- **LNG terminals**: Qatar's Ras Laffan complex (world's largest LNG export
  facility), UAE Das Island, Oman LNG.

### Chokepoints / routes
- **Strait of Hormuz** — 21 miles wide, 21km navigable channel; the central
  parameter of this model.
- **Suez Canal** — alternative for oil heading to Europe; adds ~7 days and
  significant cost; has its own capacity limits.
- **Cape of Good Hope** — full alternative for Asia-bound oil; adds ~2–3 weeks.
- **Saudi East-West Pipeline** (Petroline) — 1.2 Mbpd capacity; bypasses Hormuz
  for Saudi oil headed to the Red Sea. Currently the most important safety valve.
- **Iraq-Turkey (Kirkuk-Ceyhan) Pipeline** — 0.35 Mbpd; bypasses Hormuz for
  Iraqi oil to Mediterranean. Damaged and intermittently operational.
- **Abu Dhabi-Fujairah Pipeline** — 1.5 Mbpd; UAE bypass to Gulf of Oman coast.
  The most strategically significant recent investment.

### Major destination ports and refineries
Key refinery clusters: Rotterdam (Europe's largest), Ulsan/Yeosu (South Korea),
Jamnagar (India, world's largest single complex), Jurong (Singapore), Chiba
(Japan), Tianjin (China). These are the consumption-side endpoints whose supply
is disrupted.

Each of these has a known lat/lon and can be a geographic node in the simulation
and on the visualization globe.

## Agent taxonomy

### Tier 1: Infrastructure agents (geographically located)
These are initialized from reference data and are largely static:

- `OilField` — production capacity (Mbpd), current output (% of capacity), operator
- `GasField` — similar; distinguishes pipeline gas vs LNG
- `Port` — lat/lon, commodity types handled, daily throughput capacity, current congestion
- `Refinery` — lat/lon, crude input capacity, output products, current utilization
- `Pipeline` — origin/destination nodes, capacity (Mbpd), current operational status
- `FertilizerPlant` — lat/lon, input requirements (natural gas, sulfur), output capacity, current status
- `StorageFacility` — strategic petroleum reserve, tank farm; capacity + current fill level

### Tier 2: Market agents (pricing and routing logic)
- `CrudeOilMarket` — Brent/WTI/Dubai Mercantile spot prices; demand/supply balance
- `LNGMarket` — JKM (Japan-Korea Marker) spot price; regional differentiation
- `FertilizerMarket` — urea, ammonia, phosphate prices; seasonal demand curves
- `InsuranceMarket` — war-risk premium as a function of blockade intensity and
  incident rate; this is the most non-obvious agent: even a 30% physical blockade
  can generate an effective 90%+ commercial blockade via uninsurable risk
- `ShippingMarket` — Baltic Dry Index / tanker day rates; derived from supply/demand
  for vessel capacity on available routes

### Tier 3: Geopolitical scenario parameters (not modelled as agents)
Deliberately treated as **parameters, not agents**, to avoid political modelling:
- `BlockadeIntensity` (0–1) — physical effectiveness of Iranian maritime interdiction
- `InsuranceMultiplier` — amplification factor from insurance freeze
- `USNavyEscortCoverage` (0–1) — fraction of commercial traffic that can be escorted
- `PipelineBypassCapacity` — how much of East-West, Fujairah pipelines are online

The user sets these parameters (or the simulation steps them over time) and the
model shows consequences without simulating the decision-making of any state actor.

### Tier 4: Consumer/impact agents (second-order effects)
- `Country` — the core second-order agent:
  - Import dependency coefficients: oil, LNG, urea, ammonia, sulfur, helium
  - Strategic reserve levels (oil: IEA mandates 90-day supply; fertilizer: minimal)
  - Population size, food import dependency, GDP per capita
  - Current stress state: derived from commodity price × import dependency
- `FarmingRegion` — optional sub-country resolution: fertilizer application rate
  as a function of input price; yield loss as a function of reduced application
  using FAO elasticity coefficients
- `FertilizerPlant` — demand for natural gas input; shuts down when LNG price
  exceeds threshold (real shutdowns observed in India, Bangladesh, Pakistan)

## Aggregate flow model vs. geographic granularity

The user's question: can we have both an aggregate flow model AND geographic
visualization? Yes — and these are not in tension. The architecture is:

1. **Aggregate commodity flows** as the computational model: oil flows from
   Saudi fields to Rotterdam at X Mbpd, disrupted to Y Mbpd when Hormuz is
   at Z% blockade. The arithmetic is simple and fast.

2. **Geographic nodes** as visualization anchors: each flow has a real origin
   point (Ras Tanura, lat 26.64°N, lon 50.17°E) and a real destination
   (Rotterdam, lat 51.89°N, lon 4.14°E). The arc drawn on the globe between
   them is the visualization of an aggregate flow — its thickness and brightness
   represent the volume, its color the commodity type.

This gives lay users exactly what they want — visual confirmation that there are
real oil fields, real refineries, real chokepoints — without requiring individual
tanker simulation. The nodes are real, the flows are real (initialized from UN
Comtrade bilateral trade data), and the disruption is parameterized.

**Visualization intent (for later implementation):**
- 3D globe (extending the existing Three.js Globe component)
- Arcs between origin and destination nodes: radius proportional to flow volume,
  animated particles moving along arcs
- Node markers: oil fields (amber), LNG terminals (blue), refineries (orange),
  ports (white), fertilizer plants (green), strategic reserves (grey)
- Chokepoint markers: pulsing ring at Hormuz, Suez, Cape; size = current
  blockade intensity
- Country fill: choropleth coloring by stress index (food security, economic
  impact)
- Time control: slider from pre-crisis baseline through escalation to various
  resolution scenarios

Individual tanker visualization would be a later enhancement using AIS data —
interesting but not required for the model to produce meaningful and visually
compelling results.

## Other modelling efforts to learn from

**FAO/AMIS** — publishes methodology for fertilizer shock cascades. Their approach
is input-output (static): fertilizer price → application rate reduction (elasticity
~0.3–0.6) → yield reduction → food price increase → food insecurity. Scavengeable:
the elasticity coefficients and their country-level vulnerability indices.

**Kpler / Vortexa** — the most operationally relevant. They track individual
vessel movements and publish aggregate commodity flow analytics. Their output is
what journalists cite. They don't publish models, but the data is commercially
available and some aggregate statistics appear in free reports.

**S&P Global Commodity Insights** (Platts/IHS Markit) — most sophisticated
commercial oil flow models. Methodology described in accessible terms in their
market reports. The structure: supply nodes → route nodes → demand nodes, with
price-clearing at each step.

**GTAP (Global Trade Analysis Project)** — the academic workhorse for trade shock
analysis. A CGE (Computable General Equilibrium) model covering country × sector
bilateral trade. The GTAP database gives us the input-output coefficients (how
much oil goes into fertilizer production, how much fertilizer goes into agriculture)
that should seed the Country agents. The model itself is heavy, but its structure
is directly applicable.

**Oxford Economics / Capital Economics** — publish macro shock models for oil
price spikes. Their GDP elasticity estimates (% GDP impact per $10/barrel oil
price increase) are useful calibration inputs.

The gap all these efforts share: none of them are interactive. FAO publishes
a PDF. Kpler publishes a dashboard readable by analysts. GTAP runs in a
specialized software package. The Orbital simulation makes the same underlying
mechanics explorable by anyone.

## The general second-order model concept

Both IPCC warming and Hormuz closure converge on the same second-order variables:
- Food price index
- Fuel price index
- Fertilizer availability
- Economic stress
- Population vulnerability

The architectural insight: rather than duplicate the human-impact modeling in
each scenario, build a **Global Stress Layer** as a reusable module:

```
Scenario A (IPCC warming)     ─┐
Scenario B (Hormuz closure)   ─┼─▶  [ Global Stress Layer ]  ─▶  Population outcomes
Scenario C (future scenario)  ─┘
```

The stress layer takes commodity price indices and availability signals as
inputs and produces food insecurity levels, migration pressure, and political
stability indicators by country. FAO and WFP publish calibration data for this
layer (their food insecurity thresholds and price-hunger elasticities).

This generalises the simulation engine: the Hormuz model is one "forcing" applied
to a general global system, exactly as CO₂ forcing is applied to the climate system.

## On tipping points and meaningful feedback

The user raises the hardest problem: connecting quantitative outputs (5°C warming,
80% fertilizer price increase) to human meaning (deaths, migration, collapse).

Thresholds that matter — informed by existing frameworks:
- **Food insecurity threshold**: WFP uses a food basket cost index; above ~30%
  price increase, acute hunger risk rises sharply in high-vulnerability countries
- **Nitrogen fertilizer application**: below ~60% of normal application, cereal
  yields typically drop 20–40% (FAO elasticity data)
- **Oil price spike**: each $20/barrel sustained increase correlates to ~0.5%
  global GDP reduction (IMF estimates)
- **State fragility**: the Fund for Peace Fragile States Index includes food/fuel
  price shocks as explicit inputs; there are historical correlations between price
  spikes and political instability events

These thresholds can be modelled as tipping functions in Country agents —
continuous responses that cross visible thresholds at calibrated values.

## Open questions for planning

- [ ] What is the time resolution? Daily for the crisis acute phase (tanker
  movements), monthly for second-order effects (farming, hunger)?
- [ ] Do we model the current actual 2026 crisis (needing real recent data) or
  a parameterized generic Hormuz closure scenario (more flexible, less data work)?
  Suggest: parameterized scenario with real reference infrastructure, validated
  against observed price data.
- [ ] What is the first visible output? Suggest: world map with country-level
  oil-import stress coloring, plus commodity price time series. Fast to build,
  immediately legible.
- [ ] Database question: UN Comtrade data for ~200 countries × ~20 commodity types
  is a few MB as JSON — still filesystem-tractable. Hold on MongoDB until the
  reference data volume or real-time update requirements demand it.
- [ ] How to handle the political sensitivity? Framing: conditional scenario
  analysis ("if blockade at 80% for 6 months..."), historical precedent anchor
  (1980s Tanker Wars), no state actor blame, transparent model assumptions.

## Proposed build sequence

**Phase 1 — Static geography + commodity flows**
- Load infrastructure nodes from Global Energy Monitor + World Port Index
- Load country import dependency from UN Comtrade + World Bank
- Build commodity flow network (producers → routes → consumers)
- Parameterize Hormuz blockade intensity
- Output: country-level price impact + stress index

**Phase 2 — Second-order cascades**
- Fertilizer price → application rate → yield reduction (FAO elasticities)
- Food price → food insecurity by country (WFP thresholds)
- Strategic reserve depletion over time

**Phase 3 — Geographic visualization**
- Globe arcs for commodity flows (volume = thickness/brightness, commodity = color)
- Node markers for infrastructure (fields, ports, refineries, plants)
- Country choropleth for stress index
- Time slider / blockade intensity control

**Phase 4 — Validation + AIS data**
- Validate commodity price trajectories against observed 2026 price data
- Optionally layer in AIS tanker data for strait transit visualization

## What was built (June 5 2026)

Six agents, one `BLOCKADE` parameter (0–1), full cascade falls out from the structure:

```
BLOCKADE → flows.js → energy.js → fertilizer.js → agriculture.js → food-security.js
                    ↘ helium.js
```

### Agent responsibilities

**`flows.js`** — commodity flow network. The only agent that reads the `blockade`
parameter. Computes actual flow rate for each commodity as:

```
flow_rate = bypass_capacity × ramp_factor + (1 - bypass_capacity) × (1 - blockade)
```

Bypass routes (East-West Pipeline, Fujairah, Cape) ramp up over 30–120 days as
operators find alternatives. LNG and helium have minimal bypass — no pipelines
and Cape routing is too slow. Tracks cumulative stranded volume.

**`energy.js`** — oil (Brent) and LNG (JKM) spot prices from supply/demand
imbalance. Includes strategic reserve buffer (90-day IEA stock absorbs some
initial pressure). Oil price elasticity ~4× (empirical), LNG ~6× (more volatile).

**`fertilizer.js`** — ammonia/urea plant utilization. Plants shut down when
LNG price exceeds ~$22/MMBtu (gas is ~70% of Haber-Bosch production cost).
At $35+, only 10% of capacity remains. Sulfur shortage (50% seaborne from
Gulf) compounds by cutting phosphate production.

**`agriculture.js`** — farmer application response to fertilizer price.
Price-sensitive markets (South/Southeast Asia) reduce N application; yield
follows via FAO elasticities (~0.42 for wheat). A 30% N reduction → ~12%
yield decline.

**`food-security.js`** — country-level WFP threshold model. Reads global
food production index and oil price (distribution cost channel). Applies
country-specific vulnerability multipliers (SPR days, import dependency).
Outputs insecurity level 0–4 and people at risk.

**`helium.js`** — unique boiloff mechanic. Qatar goes offline when LNG flow
drops below 15%. Stranded containers start 45-day clock; helium evaporates
permanently. Downstream scores for semiconductor fabs (TSMC/Samsung/SK Hynix)
and MRI availability.

### Observed outputs at BLOCKADE=0.8 (12 weeks)

| Metric | Baseline | Week 1 | Week 12 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Oil flow | 100% | 25% | 42% (bypass ramp) |
| LNG flow | 100% | 20% | 22% |
| Brent crude | $80/bbl | $281/bbl | $246/bbl |
| LNG-JKM | $12/MMBtu | $69.5/MMBtu | $68/MMBtu |
| Fertilizer plants | 100% | 10% | 10% |
| Urea price | 1.0× | 1.92× | 1.92× |
| Wheat yield | 100% | 85% | 85% |
| People at risk | 0M | 1000M | 458M |
| Most stressed | — | Bangladesh(4), Pakistan(4) | Bangladesh(3), Pakistan(3) |

The initial spike (week 1) is extreme; SPR drawdown and bypass ramp moderates
the oil price over subsequent weeks. The fertilizer and agriculture effects are
persistent (plants don't restart quickly).

**Calibration needed:** Brent at $281/bbl with 80% blockade is probably too high.
The Commodity Context article documents 12-14% of global oil lost over 3 months —
implying a more modest effective disruption. Price elasticity of 4.0 should be
verified against observed price data from the current crisis.

## Geo-intelligence data wishlist

The simulation data files are a start, but the project could be significantly
enriched by ingesting real-world signals. A taxonomy:

### Tier 1: Immediately needed (for visualization)

**Natural Earth country borders** — the most immediate gap.
GeoJSON/TopoJSON country outlines at 1:50m or 1:10m resolution.
Public domain. Required for the globe overlay to show recognizable countries.
https://www.naturalearthdata.com/downloads/50m-cultural-vectors/
https://github.com/nvkelso/natural-earth-vector (pre-converted GeoJSON)

**Natural Earth populated places** — cities with population, coordinates.
Enables showing human exposure: which cities are in high-stress countries,
rough population concentrations.
https://www.naturalearthdata.com/downloads/50m-cultural-vectors/

**Maritime shipping lanes** — routes ships actually take through and around
the Gulf. The IMO Traffic Separation Schemes define the Hormuz lane geometry.
OpenStreetMap has maritime routes. Needed for the arc visualization to follow
realistic paths rather than great-circle lines.
https://www.marinetraffic.com/en/ais/details/ (for reference)
https://www.imo.org/en/OurWork/Safety/Pages/ShipRouteing.aspx

### Tier 2: High value for calibration and signal

**EIA/OPEC production data** — monthly oil production by country.
Free. Allows initialization of the flows model from real numbers rather than
estimates, and real-time recalibration as production changes.
https://www.eia.gov/international/data/world

**AMIS Market Monitor** — fertilizer and food commodity spot prices monthly.
Free. Essential for calibrating the fertilizer and food cascade agents.
Ground truth for whether the model's price predictions match observed prices.
https://www.amis-outlook.org/amis-about/market-monitor

**World Bank Commodity Price Data** — Brent, LNG, urea, ammonia, phosphate
price time series. Free. Calibration anchor for energy and fertilizer agents.
https://www.worldbank.org/en/research/commodity-markets

**ACLED (Armed Conflict Location and Event Data)** — real-time conflict
events globally, each with coordinates, date, actor, type, fatalities.
Free API for non-commercial research. Immediately applicable to Hormuz: shows
actual IRGC incidents, naval events, drone strikes in the Gulf with
lat/lon and dates. Enables event-driven scenario updates.
https://acleddata.com/

**AIS vessel tracking** — Automatic Identification System positions for
commercial vessels. Pre-crisis: ~150 large ships/day through Hormuz.
Historical data (2024–2026) would show the actual traffic collapse.
MarineTraffic free tier gives current positions; historical requires API.
For Hormuz specifically, the 21-mile-wide strait means all vessels
are in a known corridor — very tractable to extract.
https://www.marinetraffic.com/

### Tier 3: Second-order social and political signals

**Fragile States Index (Fund for Peace)** — annual composite score per country
covering economic decline, state legitimacy, human rights, security apparatus.
Free download. Allows the food-security agent to weight crisis risk more
accurately: a 1.3× food price increase in Bangladesh (FSI score 80) is
more dangerous than the same increase in Japan (FSI score 25).
https://fragilestatesindex.org/

**Global Hunger Index** — annual hunger scores by country; combines
undernourishment, child wasting, child stunting, child mortality.
Free. Provides the pre-crisis baseline food security state that the model
perturbs — more accurate than our current flat vulnerability assumption.
https://www.globalhungerindex.org/

**WFP Vulnerability Analysis and Mapping (VAM)** — operational food security
monitoring data from the field. Free access. More granular than GHI; updated
more frequently during active crises.
https://www.wfp.org/publications/vam

**UNHCR refugee/displacement data** — population movements by country.
When food insecurity or conflict reaches crisis level, displacement follows.
Could eventually be a downstream output of the simulation.
https://www.unhcr.org/refugee-statistics/

**V-Dem (Varieties of Democracy)** — political data for 200 countries:
regime type, electoral integrity, civil liberties. Free. Useful for
modeling state response to economic shock: autocratic states may restrict
food exports; democratic states may have different crisis response.
https://v-dem.net/

### Tier 4: Environmental and agricultural ground truth

**Copernicus Land Service / MODIS** — satellite-derived land cover,
vegetation indices (NDVI), crop condition monitoring.
Free (Copernicus), near-real-time. Would allow the agriculture agent
to be initialized from actual crop condition rather than FAO baselines —
if drought is already stressing crops, the fertilizer shock compounds.
https://land.copernicus.eu/global/products/ndvi

**ERA5 (ECMWF reanalysis)** — global atmospheric reanalysis at hourly,
daily, monthly resolution since 1940. Free. Provides the weather context
for any agricultural scenario: precipitation, temperature, soil moisture.
Allows coupling the IPCC warming scenario to the Hormuz food scenario
(double shock: crops already stressed by heat, then fertilizer price spike).
https://cds.climate.copernicus.eu/

**FAOSTAT** — crop production, trade volumes, area harvested by country
and crop. Free API. Ground truth for the agriculture agent: actual
pre-crisis yields, planting areas, consumption by crop type.
https://www.fao.org/faostat/

**USDA WASDE** — monthly World Agricultural Supply and Demand Estimates.
The US government's global crop supply/demand balance sheets.
Free, monthly. Provides the supply-demand context for how much a yield
reduction would actually move food prices.
https://www.usda.gov/oce/commodity/wasde/

### On signals vs. facts

The existing simulation is initialized from **facts** (production capacity,
import dependency). What would make it significantly more powerful is
**signals** — time-varying data streams that update the model's starting
state to reflect current reality.

The ideal architecture: a scheduled job fetches key signal feeds daily
or weekly (AMIS prices, EIA production, ACLED events) and updates the
scenario's initialization data. The model then runs forward from current
observed conditions rather than a historical baseline.

This is the difference between "what would happen if Hormuz closed"
and "given current conditions (today's prices, current production levels,
this week's ACLED incidents), what does the cascade look like from here."
The second is far more useful and far more interesting.

The storage question: signals need a time dimension. The filesystem approach
works for static reference data; time-series signals eventually need a
database or at minimum dated JSON snapshots. SQLite would be adequate for
the volume involved (a few MB per year of daily commodity prices).

## New concepts introduced — June 9 2026

*These ideas emerged from thinking about what the Hormuz simulation is missing:
a web visualization, a connection to real events, and the bridge from model
outputs to human meaning. They are general features of any serious simulation,
not Hormuz-specific.*

---

### The cascade chain problem

The simulation currently produces numbers: Brent at $241/bbl, urea at 1.92×,
458M people at food risk. These are correct but they are **latent** — they exist
inside the model and in the console output, but the causal chain connecting them
is invisible to a user. Someone reading the numbers doesn't understand why they
follow from each other, or which links in the chain are the critical ones.

Making the cascade explicit is not just a visualization problem — it is an
epistemological one. The simulation's core claim is that A causes B causes C.
If a user can't see that claim, they can't interrogate it, and the simulation
is no different from a black-box prediction.

**The design principle:** every simulation interface should show the causal
chain as a chain, not as isolated outputs. For Hormuz:

```
Blockade intensity (80%)
  → Oil flow (25% of baseline)
  → Brent crude ($241/bbl, +201%)
  → LNG-JKM ($68/MMBtu, +467%)
  → Fertilizer plants (10% utilization — gas too expensive for Haber-Bosch)
  → Urea price (+92%)
  → Farmer N-application (72% of optimal)
  → Wheat yield (85% of baseline)
  → Food price index (+18% in Bangladesh)
  → Food insecurity level 3 (crisis) — 458M people affected
```

Each arrow is a claim. Each number is a claim. The interface should make every
claim inspectable: why does 80% blockade produce $241/bbl? What is the elasticity
assumption? Can the user change it?

This is the participatory simulation argument: not just showing results but
exposing the machinery that produces them.

---

### Event injection — hardcoded vs. data-driven

The simulation currently has one parameter: `BLOCKADE` (0–1), set at startup.
This is useful for "what if" analysis but does not capture how a real crisis
unfolds — with discrete events at specific times, partial recoveries, escalations,
and side effects that don't fit a smooth ramp.

**The event timeline pattern** extends the manifest with a sequence of shocks:

```javascript
export const timeline = {
  inherits: '@orbital/agents/hormuz/timeline.js',
  events: [
    { week: 0,  type: 'blockade_change',   value: 0.0,  note: 'Strait open — baseline' },
    { week: 4,  type: 'blockade_change',   value: 0.5,  note: 'Iranian mines deployed' },
    { week: 8,  type: 'blockade_change',   value: 0.85, note: 'Full IRGC interdiction' },
    { week: 10, type: 'facility_shock',    id: 'ras_laffan', factor: 0.17, note: 'Missile strike on LNG complex' },
    { week: 14, type: 'route_degradation', route: 'suez_canal', factor: 0.7, note: 'Houthi missile threat intensifies' },
    { week: 20, type: 'blockade_change',   value: 0.4,  note: 'US Navy corridor established' },
  ],
};
```

The `timeline` agent fires `{ shock: { type, id, value, factor } }` bus events
at the right ticks. Each simulation agent handles `event.shock` and adjusts its
state accordingly. The `flows.js` agent handles `blockade_change` and
`route_degradation`; a future `facilities.js` agent handles `facility_shock`.

This pattern decouples *what happens* from *how agents respond*. The timeline is
pure data — editable, shareable, inspectable.

**Two modes of timeline:**

**Hardcoded (scripted scenario):** The events array is hand-authored in the
manifest. This is the right starting point — it's fast, explicit, and lets you
design specific scenario arcs (escalation, partial ceasefire, full resolution)
without depending on external data quality. Most published scenario analyses
(IEA, FAO) work this way.

**Data-driven (event-grounded scenario):** The timeline is generated from the
ACLED/GDELT event database. A query retrieves Gulf conflict events since a
start date; a classifier maps each event to a shock `{ type, magnitude, duration }`:

```
ACLED: "IRGC fast boat attacked tanker in Gulf of Oman"
  → { type: 'blockade_change', delta: +0.08, decay_weeks: 3 }

ACLED: "US 5th Fleet announced escort operation, 70 transits"
  → { type: 'escort_coverage', delta: +0.15, decay_weeks: 8 }

GDELT: "Ras Laffan LNG complex reported fire"
  → { type: 'facility_shock', id: 'ras_laffan', factor: 0.83, duration_weeks: 4 }
```

The classifier is initially rule-based (keyword + actor matching), eventually
could be improved with LLM classification. The output is the same timeline
format — the simulation doesn't know whether its events were hand-written or
database-derived.

The **practical path**: start with hardcoded timelines that model the general
arc of the current crisis (open → partial blockade → full blockade → partial
reopening). Build the data-driven classifier as a second step once the event
database is well populated. Both modes should be visible to the user — "you
are running scenario: 2026 Hormuz Crisis (scripted)" vs. "2026 Hormuz Crisis
(ACLED-grounded, events from Jan 1 – Jun 9)."

### Tipping points — where the fabric tears

A tipping point is a threshold in a continuous variable below which social
systems are resilient and above which they undergo rapid, often irreversible
change. The simulation currently produces continuous outputs (food insecurity
level 3.2, stress index 0.74). What it does not yet show is where the
**qualitative transitions** are.

**The food price signal is the most empirically grounded.**
Research on the Arab Spring (2010–11) showed that the FAO Food Price Index
crossing approximately 210 (on their scale, where 100 = 2014–2016 average)
preceded civil unrest with a 6–12 month lag. This is not a theoretical claim —
it is a finding from data. The model can encode this directly:

```
food_price_index < 180:   stable — population absorbs the cost
180–210:                  stressed — reduced consumption, protest risk rising
210–240:                  acute — high protest probability, fragile governments at risk
> 240:                    emergency — historical precedent for rapid political collapse
```

**Fragility as a multiplier.** The Fragile States Index gives each country a
baseline fragility score (1–120). The same food price increase has very different
consequences in Japan (FSI ~20) versus Yemen (FSI ~110). The tipping threshold is
lower for fragile states — they have less cushion. A working model:

```
effective_threshold = base_threshold / (1 + fsf_multiplier)
where fsf_multiplier = (country.fsi / 60) × 0.5
```

A country with FSI 120 has its tipping threshold halved relative to one with FSI 0.

**The nonlinear response.** Real tipping points are not proportional — countries
absorb stress up to a point and then collapse rapidly. The right model is a
**sigmoid response function** rather than a linear one:

```
instability_risk = 1 / (1 + exp(-k × (stress - threshold)))
```

Where `k` controls the sharpness of the transition. This gives a near-zero
probability below the threshold and a near-certain probability above it, with
a relatively narrow transition band — which matches how political crises actually
look from the outside (long apparent stability, then sudden collapse).

**What the model does and doesn't claim:**
The model can signal "Bangladesh has crossed the food security crisis threshold
and its fragility score suggests significant instability risk within 3–6 months."
It cannot predict who wins the resulting political conflict, what form the
instability takes, or whether a particular government falls. The output is a risk
signal, not a political forecast. This boundary matters for responsible framing.

### The latent→tangible bridge — a general principle

The Hormuz cascade produces: Brent at $241, urea at 1.92×, 458M at risk.
These numbers are correct but they remain abstract to most readers.

The bridge from the latent space of a model to human-comprehensible consequences
requires two steps:

**1. Calibrated thresholds:** At what value does the output become meaningful?
Not "urea is at 1.92×" but "urea is at a level where fertilizer plants in
Bangladesh and Pakistan have shut down (this has already happened in the real
crisis)." The threshold gives the number meaning.

**2. Concrete human referents:** Not "458M at risk" but "this is approximately
the combined population of the United States, Brazil, and Canada — all of whom
would face acute food insecurity." Or: "the last time global food insecurity
rose by this amount, it coincided with the 2011 Arab Spring."

Neither of these requires new modeling — they require connecting model outputs
to real-world events and known quantities that a non-specialist can visualize.

**The helium case is the sharpest example of this principle.** The boiloff
counter is not interesting as "47 MMcm of helium evaporated." It is interesting
as "the equivalent of 3 months of South Korean semiconductor production capacity
has been permanently removed from the supply chain, and TSMC has announced
production cuts." The model knows the volume; the interface should translate it
into the consequence that a person actually cares about.

**This principle generalizes across all simulations:**

| Raw model output | Human-tangible equivalent |
|---|---|
| ΔT = 3.2°C global | Arctic warms 10°C; coral bleaching at 90% of reefs; wet-bulb 35°C days tripled in South Asia |
| Urea at 1.92× | Spring planting season in Bangladesh — 40% of farmers reduce application; harvest down 12% |
| Helium: 47 MMcm destroyed | Samsung and SK Hynix announce production cuts; TSMC issues force majeure to Apple |
| FSI threshold crossed | Historical analog: Tunisia 2011 → Ben Ali fled in 28 days |

Building a library of these translations — model output → human referent — is
not the simulation's job. It is the interface layer's job, and it requires
domain knowledge and careful calibration. It also has to be done without
overclaiming causality: "this level of food insecurity has historically been
associated with political instability" is defensible; "this will cause a
revolution" is not.

### What the web visualization needs to show

Given the above, the Hormuz web page needs:

**1. The timeline control** — not just a blockade slider but a time axis. The
user can move forward through a scenario week by week and watch the cascade
develop. Optionally: play/pause, like the world-events globe.

**2. The cascade as a visible chain** — a column of connected indicators:
`blockade → oil flow → energy prices → fertilizer → crops → food → stability`.
Each indicator shows its current value and the threshold label (stable/stressed/
crisis/emergency). The arrows between them make causality visible.

**3. The world map** — choropleth by country stress/instability risk. The
countries that cross thresholds turn red. The 458M number gets a face: it's
Bangladesh, Pakistan, Nigeria, the Philippines.

**4. The helium clock** — a separate counter showing cumulative permanent supply
destruction and the downstream semiconductor/MRI impact score.

**5. Threshold markers on all charts** — horizontal lines at the calibrated
thresholds (FAO food price 210, FSI-adjusted instability threshold). When the
simulation's line crosses the threshold marker, something meaningful is happening.

**6. The event feed** — if running in data-driven mode, a sidebar showing which
ACLED/GDELT events triggered which shocks. This is transparency about what drove
the scenario.

### Immediate build sequence (revised)

**Step 1: Timeline agent** — a small bus agent that reads the `events` array
from the manifest and fires `{ shock: {...} }` at the right ticks. Each
existing agent (flows, energy, fertilizer) handles `event.shock` and adjusts
its state. This is the bridge from scripted scenarios to data-driven ones.

**Step 2: Shock handlers in agents** — add `if (event.shock?.type === ...)` to
each agent's `resolve()`. This is a few lines per agent.

**Step 3: Hardcoded 2026 scenario** — author a timeline in the manifest that
models the actual arc of the current crisis (open → mines deployed → full
interdiction → partial corridor). This is the "what actually happened" mode
without needing the ACLED classifier yet.

**Step 4: Web page** — `public/orbital/hormuz/index.html`. Cascade chain panel,
world map, helium clock, timeline slider. Uses the existing sim API and socket
protocol. The IPCC page is the template.

**Step 5: ACLED→timeline classifier** — a query that reads Gulf events from the
SQLite DB, classifies them into shocks, and produces a timeline automatically.
This enables "ACLED-grounded" mode.

**Step 6: Threshold library** — a small data file mapping model outputs to
human-tangible referents, with source citations. Used by the interface to
annotate values with context.
