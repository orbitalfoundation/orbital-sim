One way of thinking about what I'm trying to solve here is that there is a computability problem, both in terms of velocity and size of issues, and information opaqueness. Diffuse beneficiaries, deferred costs and so on escape our colloquial rhetoric based and overly simplistic voting models. Basically we just are not reasoning very well as groups. There are in fact several asymmetrical situations: who holds which piece of the picture, and why the pieces don't add up in any single parties head:

1. Expert–lay asymmetry. One side has a model (the city engineer's pipe-capacity spreadsheet) and the other has lived experience. The technical account is correct but illegible; the experiential account is legible but partial. Stormwater, structural seismic risk, grid load. The communication failure here is translation.

2. Distributional engagement asymmetry. The cost and the benefit land on different people. Concentrated cost, diffuse benefit - a bike lane removes 12 parking spots (those 12 people show up furious) to serve 3,000 cyclists (who don't show up because each gains a little). The information problem is that the diffuse beneficiaries can't see their own stake, so the process systematically overweights the concentrated loser.

3. Temporal asymmetry. Cost now, benefit later, or the reverse. Deferred maintenance, pension liabilities, wildfire fuel reduction, transit capital. Humans and political cycles both discount the future steeply, so the deferred term is structurally invisible at decision time. Hard to prove or show counter factuals.

4. Absent-stakeholder problem. In housing and zoning, the most affected party (the person who would live there if the units existed) isn't in the room, can't vote, and has no representation. Same with future generations on climate, future patients on health siting. The asymmetry isn't that information is hidden; it's that a whole class of stakeholders may not even exist yet, have no channel to express interest at all. A model can give them a voice by making their interest computable. The same is true for non-human stakeholders

5. Coupling asymmetry. Citizens experience issues siloed single voting topic issues ("traffic is bad," "rent is high," "the school's overcrowded"). But those are often side-effects or outputs of one coupled system. No single stakeholder sees the links, and each proposed fix to one silo silently loads the others. If participants can playtest different scenarios they see consequences better across the board.

6. Competing goals and strategic asymmetry. Some stakeholders have incentive to misrepresent. Developer lowballs traffic impact; opponent inflates it. People distort numbers - creates distrust. Shared model can at least be inspected - even if inputs are biases.

7. Emotionally radioactive issues. Some issues are simply hard to discuss - people shy away from them. This can make those issues visible in a sensitive and private way.
