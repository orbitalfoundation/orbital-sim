Representation Paradigms
System Dynamics (SD) is probably the most natural fit for what you're describing. You model the world as stocks (inventories, populations, reserves) and flows (production rates, consumption, transport) connected by feedback loops. The Strait of Hormuz scenario maps cleanly: oil reserve stocks → refinery throughput flows → fertilizer production stocks → crop yield flows → food price feedback loops. SD captures delays and nonlinear feedback well, which is where the interesting emergent behavior lives.
Agent-Based Models (ABM) complement this when you want discrete actors — countries, firms, ships — making decisions under uncertainty. An oil tanker rerouting around the Cape of Good Hope is an agent behavior, not a stock-and-flow.
Network/Graph Models are the natural representation for supply chains themselves. Nodes are facilities or regions, edges are trade routes with capacity and cost attributes. Graph topology matters enormously — you want to find chokepoints (Hormuz, Suez, Panama, Taiwan Strait) and measure cascade failure risk across them.
Computable General Equilibrium (CGE) models are what economists use for exactly this — modeling how price shocks propagate through interconnected sectors. GTAP (Global Trade Analysis Project) is the dominant framework. Very powerful but extremely data-heavy and somewhat opaque.
Input-Output (I-O) Analysis is the Leontief model — a matrix where every sector's output is another's input. Wassily Leontief built this in the 1940s and it's still the backbone of how economists trace upstream/downstream impacts. Simple to reason about, easy to shock (zero out a row representing Hormuz oil), and the propagation math is just matrix inversion. This is probably your quickest path to meaningful answers.

Tooling Ecosystem
For system dynamics, Vensim is the industry standard (used in climate and pandemic modeling), Stella Architect is more accessible, and PySD lets you run Vensim models in Python. The World3 model (Limits to Growth) is open-source and a useful starting template.
For agent-based, NetLogo is the classic academic tool; Mesa is the Python equivalent. GAMA is strong for spatial/geographic agent models which matters for your geographic flows.
For supply chain network modeling, there's a cluster around operations research tools — AnyLogic uniquely combines SD, ABM, and discrete-event simulation in one environment and is probably the most used commercial tool for exactly your use case. OR-Tools from Google handles the optimization side.
For I-O and economic modeling, Python with pandas/numpy is fine for basic Leontief, but GTAP and EXIOBASE (a multi-region I-O database covering 200 countries × 200 sectors) are the data foundations worth knowing about. EXIOBASE in particular is remarkable — it includes environmental extensions (CO₂, water, land use) so you can trace ecosystem impacts through supply chains.
For uncertainty and scenario analysis, Monte Carlo simulation layered over any of the above is standard. Exploratory Modeling and Analysis (EMA Workbench, a Python library) is specifically designed for deep uncertainty — exactly your "speculative storms and wars" use case — using techniques like scenario discovery and robust decision-making.

Data Sources
The hardest part is data, not modeling. The key ones: UN Comtrade for trade flows, IEA for energy, FAOSTAT for agriculture, the Observatory of Economic Complexity (OEC) for beautifully structured product-level trade data, and USGS for mineral supply chains. For geospatial infrastructure, Global Fishing Watch and OpenStreetMap-derived datasets cover ports, pipelines, and rail.

What I'd Actually Recommend for Your Starting Point
The I-O matrix approach with EXIOBASE as your data foundation, implemented in Python, gets you to something meaningful fastest. You can model shocks by zeroing or reducing entries (Hormuz closes → Iranian oil row goes to zero), then propagate the impact matrix through the economy. Layer Monte Carlo on top for your speculative scenarios. Once you have that working, you'd know which sectors need richer dynamics (where SD becomes worth adding) and which actors matter enough for an ABM layer.
AnyLogic is worth knowing about if you want a single integrated environment — it's used by defense contractors and logistics companies for exactly the kind of global cascade modeling you're describing.
